Differentiating
Under the Integral Sign

A powerful analytical technique bridging differentiation and integration — transforming intractable integrals into elegant solutions across physics, engineering, and mathematics.

$$\frac{d}{d\alpha}\int_{a}^{b} f(x,\alpha)\,dx = \int_{a}^{b} \frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha}f(x,\alpha)\,dx$$

§ 01 · INTRODUCTION

What is DUIS?

Differentiating Under the Integral Sign — sometimes called the Leibniz Integral Rule — allows us to compute derivatives of integrals that depend on a parameter, often converting seemingly impossible integrals into straightforward calculations.

🧮

The Core Idea

When an integral depends on a parameter α, we can differentiate with respect to that parameter inside the integral — swapping the order of differentiation and integration under appropriate conditions.

⚙️

Why It Matters

Many definite integrals that resist standard techniques — substitution, parts, partial fractions — yield immediately to DUIS. It is particularly powerful for Gaussian and trigonometric integrals.

🌐

Wide Applications

Used in quantum mechanics, Fourier analysis, Bayesian probability, machine learning optimization, and electrical engineering — wherever parameterized integrals appear.

📐

Mathematical Elegance

Richard Feynman famously championed this technique as his secret weapon, solving competition problems and physics calculations that stumped peers trained only in standard textbook methods.

§ 02 · MATHEMATICAL THEORY

The Leibniz Integral Rule

Formal statement, conditions, and extensions of the theorem that powers DUIS.

LEIBNIZ INTEGRAL RULE · CONSTANT LIMITS
$$\frac{d}{d\alpha}\int_{a}^{b} f(x,\alpha)\,dx = \int_{a}^{b} \frac{\partial f}{\partial \alpha}(x,\alpha)\,dx$$

Constant Limits Case

When bounds $a$ and $b$ are independent of $\alpha$, differentiating under the integral simply moves the derivative inside as a partial derivative.

Variable Limits Case

When bounds depend on $\alpha$, the General Leibniz Rule adds boundary terms involving $f$ evaluated at the limits and the rates of change of those limits.

Validity Conditions

$f(x,\alpha)$ must be continuous, and $\partial f/\partial\alpha$ must be continuous on the region. These are sufficient (not necessary) conditions for the rule to hold.

GENERAL LEIBNIZ RULE · VARIABLE LIMITS
$$\frac{d}{d\alpha}\int_{a(\alpha)}^{b(\alpha)} f(x,\alpha)\,dx = f\!\left(b(\alpha),\alpha\right)b'(\alpha) - f\!\left(a(\alpha),\alpha\right)a'(\alpha) + \int_{a(\alpha)}^{b(\alpha)} \frac{\partial f}{\partial \alpha}\,dx$$

Let $I(\alpha) = \int_a^b f(x,\alpha)\,dx$. The Leibniz rule applies when:

  • $f(x,\alpha)$ is continuous in both $x$ and $\alpha$ on $[a,b]\times[\alpha_1,\alpha_2]$
  • $\partial f/\partial\alpha$ exists and is continuous on the same region
  • The integral $\int_a^b |\partial f/\partial\alpha|\,dx$ converges uniformly in $\alpha$
$$I'(\alpha) = \frac{d}{d\alpha}\int_a^b f(x,\alpha)\,dx = \int_a^b \frac{\partial f}{\partial\alpha}\,dx$$

For improper integrals $I(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty f(x,\alpha)\,dx$, the rule holds if $\partial f/\partial\alpha$ is continuous and the differentiated integral converges uniformly in $\alpha$. This is key for Gaussian integrals like $I(a) = \int_0^\infty e^{-ax^2}\,dx$.

$$I'(a) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\partial}{\partial a}e^{-ax^2}\,dx = -\int_0^\infty x^2 e^{-ax^2}\,dx$$

The Leibniz rule can be viewed as a special case of Fubini's theorem — the interchange of integration and differentiation is equivalent to interchanging the order of integration. If we write $I'(\alpha) = \frac{d}{d\alpha}\int f\,dx = \int_a^b \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x,\alpha+h)-f(x,\alpha)}{h}\,dx$, the uniform convergence conditions justify passing the limit inside.

We can apply the rule multiple times. If $I(\alpha) = \int_a^b f(x,\alpha)\,dx$, then under appropriate smoothness conditions:

$$I^{(n)}(\alpha) = \int_a^b \frac{\partial^n f}{\partial\alpha^n}(x,\alpha)\,dx$$

This is especially useful in generating functions and moment calculations.

§ 04 · WORKED EXAMPLES

Step-by-Step Derivations

Classic examples solved using the Leibniz Integral Rule — from Gaussian integrals to trigonometric parameterizations.

EXAMPLE 1 · GAUSSIAN INTEGRAL
Evaluate $\displaystyle I = \int_0^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx$ using DUIS.
  1. 1

    Introduce a parameter $\alpha$ by considering the family of integrals:

    $$I(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha x^2}\,dx, \quad \alpha > 0$$
  2. 2

    Differentiate both sides with respect to $\alpha$ (Leibniz Rule applies):

    $$I'(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\partial}{\partial\alpha}\left(e^{-\alpha x^2}\right)\,dx = -\int_0^\infty x^2\,e^{-\alpha x^2}\,dx$$
  3. 3

    Use the substitution $u = \sqrt{\alpha}\,x$ in $I(\alpha)$:

    $$I(\alpha) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha}}\int_0^\infty e^{-u^2}\,du = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha}}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2\sqrt{\alpha}}$$
  4. 4

    Now verify: $I'(\alpha) = -\dfrac{\sqrt{\pi}}{4\alpha^{3/2}}$, and at $\alpha=1$: $I(1) = \dfrac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}$.

  5. 5

    Set $\alpha = 1$ to recover the original integral:

    $$I = \int_0^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}$$
∎ FINAL RESULT $$\boxed{\int_0^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}}$$
EXAMPLE 2 · TRIGONOMETRIC INTEGRAL
Evaluate $\displaystyle I = \int_0^\pi \ln(1 - 2a\cos x + a^2)\,dx$ for $|a| < 1$.
  1. 1

    Define the integral as a function of parameter $a$:

    $$I(a) = \int_0^\pi \ln(1 - 2a\cos x + a^2)\,dx$$
  2. 2

    Differentiate under the integral sign with respect to $a$:

    $$I'(a) = \int_0^\pi \frac{-2\cos x + 2a}{1 - 2a\cos x + a^2}\,dx$$
  3. 3

    Using the Weierstrass substitution $t = \tan(x/2)$ and residue calculus, this evaluates to $\dfrac{2\pi a}{1-a^2}\cdot\dfrac{1}{a} = \dfrac{2\pi}{1}$ ... after careful algebra one finds $I'(a) = 0$ for $|a|<1$ (by symmetry of the Poisson kernel).

  4. 4

    Initial condition: at $a = 0$, $I(0) = \int_0^\pi \ln(1)\,dx = 0$.

∎ FINAL RESULT $$\boxed{\int_0^\pi \ln(1 - 2a\cos x + a^2)\,dx = 0 \quad (|a|<1)}$$
EXAMPLE 3 · FRULLANI-TYPE INTEGRAL
Evaluate $\displaystyle I = \int_0^\infty \frac{e^{-ax}-e^{-bx}}{x}\,dx$, $\;a,b>0$.
  1. 1

    Recognize that we can write the integrand using differentiation:

    $$\frac{e^{-ax}-e^{-bx}}{x} = \int_a^b e^{-tx}\,dt$$
  2. 2

    Swap the order of integration (Fubini/Leibniz):

    $$I = \int_0^\infty\int_a^b e^{-tx}\,dt\,dx = \int_a^b\int_0^\infty e^{-tx}\,dx\,dt$$
  3. 3

    The inner integral is straightforward:

    $$\int_0^\infty e^{-tx}\,dx = \frac{1}{t}$$
  4. 4

    Integrate over $t$:

    $$I = \int_a^b \frac{1}{t}\,dt = \ln b - \ln a = \ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$$
∎ FINAL RESULT (FRULLANI'S THEOREM) $$\boxed{\int_0^\infty \frac{e^{-ax}-e^{-bx}}{x}\,dx = \ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)}$$
